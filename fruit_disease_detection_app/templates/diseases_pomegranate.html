Pomegranate Diseases


Learn about various Pomegranate fruit diseases that can affect your crop. Know about the symptoms, root causes, favorable conditions and measures to prevent the disease.


1. Bacterial leaf and fruit spot

Disease symptoms:
  • Appearance of one to several small water soaked, dark colored irregular spots on leaves resulting in premature defoliation under severe cases.

  • The pathogen also infects stem and branches causing girdling and cracking symptoms.
  • Spots on fruits were dark brown irregular slightly raised with oily appearance, which split open with L-shaped cracks under severe cases.

Root Causes:
  • Primary source of inoculum is infected cuttings.
  • Secondary source of inoculum spreads through Wind splashed rains.

Favourable Conditions:
  • The increase in day temperature (38.6°C) and afternoon relative humidity of 30.4% along with cloudy weather and intermittent rainfall favored the disease initiation and further spread of the disease.

Preventive Measures:
  • Use Disease-Free Cuttings: Ensure planting materials are free from infection.
  • Sanitation: Remove and destroy infected plant debris to reduce the inoculum.
  • Pruning: Regularly prune plants to improve air circulation and reduce humidity around the foliage.
  • Water Management: Avoid overhead irrigation; use drip irrigation to keep foliage dry.
  • Chemical Control: Apply bactericides, such as copper-based fungicides, as a preventive measure during favorable conditions.
  • Environmental Control: Monitor weather conditions and take preventive actions during periods of high humidity and rainfall.

2. Anthracnose

Disease symptoms:
  • Small, regular to irregular black spots on leaves, calyx region and fruits which turn later on as dark brown depressed spots.

  • Infected leaves turn yellow and drop off.

Root Causes:
  • Primary source of inoculum: Infected leaves.
  • Secondary source of inoculum: windborne conidia.

Favourable Conditions:
  • The disease is severe during August-September when there is high humidity, and the temperature between 20-27ºC.

Preventive Measures:
  • Use Resistant Varieties: Plant resistant or tolerant pomegranate varieties if available.
  • Sanitation:Regularly remove and destroy infected leaves and fruits.
  • Pruning: Regularly prune plants to improve air circulation and reduce humidity around the foliage.
  • Chemical Control: Use fungicides such as mancozeb or copper-based sprays at the first sign of disease.
  • Weather Monitoring: Be vigilant during periods of high humidity and moderate temperatures (20-27ºC) and apply preventive treatments accordingly.

3. Borer

Disease symptoms:
  • Holes in the pomegranate fruits and stems.

  • Presence of frass (insect excrement) around the holes.
  • Leaves and stems may wilt and die back.

Root Causes:
  • The larvae of various insect pests, such as the pomegranate fruit borer and the trunk borer.

Favourable Conditions:
  • Adult insects lay eggs in the spring and summer months.
  • Overgrown trees with dense foliage are more susceptible.

Preventive Measures:
  • Regular Monitoring:Inspect trees regularly for signs of borer activity, such as holes and frass.
  • Sanitation:Regularly remove and destroy infected leaves and fruits.
  • Pruning:Maintain proper tree shape and avoid dense foliage to reduce borer habitats.
  • Chemical Control:Use insecticides if infestation levels are high, focusing on the timing of egg-laying periods in spring and summer.
  • Biological Control: Encourage natural predators of borers, such as parasitic wasps.

4. Alternaria Blight

Disease symptoms:
  • Initially, small black spots that rapidly develop into larger, circular, sunken lesions on fruits.

  • Spores form a dark velvety layer on the infected area.
  • In severe cases, the whole fruit can rot.

Root Causes:
  • Caused by the fungus Alternaria alternata.

Favourable Conditions:
  • Warm and wet weather.
  • Mechanical damage or wounds on the fruit increase susceptibility.

Preventive Measures:
  • Sanitation: Remove and destroy infected plant parts to reduce spore levels.
  • Pruning: Prune to improve air circulation and reduce humidity around the fruit.
  • Chemical Control: Apply fungicides such as azoxystrobin or chlorothalonil at the first signs of infection.
  • Weather Monitoring: Be proactive during warm and wet weather conditions and apply preventive treatments.
  • Wound Management:Avoid mechanical damage to fruits and ensure proper handling to reduce susceptibility.

5. Wilt

Disease symptoms:
  • Yellowing and wilting of leaves and branches.

  • Dieback of branches, and eventually whole plant death.
  • Brownish discoloration of the vascular tissues inside the stem.

Root Causes:
  • Most commonly caused by the fungus Ceratocystis fimbriata. Bacterial and nematode infections can also induce wilt symptoms.

Favourable Conditions:
  • Spread easily in soil and contaminated tools.
  • Poor drainage, heavy soils, and root injuries promote infection.

Preventive Measures:
  • Use Resistant Varieties: Select and plant resistant varieties if available.
  • Soil Management: Ensure good drainage and avoid waterlogging. Use well-draining soil.
  • Sanitation: Sterilize tools to prevent spreading pathogens. Remove and destroy infected plants.
  • Crop Rotation: Avoid planting pomegranates in the same soil consecutively to reduce soil-borne pathogens.
  • Chemical Control: Use fungicides or nematicides as per extension guidelines if a specific pathogen is identified.

6. Cercospora Fruit Spot

Disease symptoms:
  • Circular brown spots with a gray center develop on fruit.


Root Causes:
  • Caused by the fungus Cercospora punicae.

Favourable Conditions:
  • Warm and humid weather.
  • Overhead irrigation that promotes prolonged leaf wetness.

Preventive Measures:
  • Sanitation: Regularly remove and destroy fallen leaves and infected fruits.
  • Pruning: Prune trees to enhance air circulation and reduce humidity.
  • Water Management: Avoid overhead irrigation to prevent prolonged leaf wetness.
  • Chemical Control: Apply fungicides such as mancozeb or copper-based sprays during periods of warm and humid weather.
  • Environmental Control: Monitor weather conditions and adjust irrigation practices to minimize leaf wetness during susceptible periods.
